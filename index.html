What is Omeka?

How many of you are familiar with Omeka? Well for those of you who aren't, here's a brief overview of where Omeka fits into the landscape of content management systems and web publishing.

Content Management System

e.g. Wordpress, Drupal, SquareSpace, etc.

In order to do that, I should talk about what a content management system is. Unsurprisingly, it's a system to help you manage your content on the web. What that means in practice is that it's the system that creates a structure around your content, your text, images, videos, etc., allowing you to pick a setup once, and then only focus on your content, and not the presentation around it.
               
A content management system is a bit like a building to house your content, in that it's a structure around your content. The structure was created for some purpose, and as such, it has different pieces of infrastructure to make certain things easier. Also like a building, content management systems exist in the context of different communities, which informs how things develop for the system itself. The original purpose of a CMS, just like the original purpose of a building, doesn't always define its current use. WordPress started as a blogging platform, and still does that quite well, but it has evolved into a platform used for all kinds of websites, from company pages to e-commerce sites. When you're looking for a way to put information on the web, you want to look at the structures that a system has to help you do stuff, and at the community of people surrounding it, to see if it's a neighborhood of people with similar or converging goals for their content.
Omeka, as a content management system, was made by and for academics, particularly in libraries, archives and museums. That background and community inform what it does and how it thinks about things. It's built around the idea of collecting various things, which you may want to describe in different ways and contextualize or use as evidence for different narratives.

Examples

So let's talk about some examples of how Omeka has been used in a classroom setting.
Using Omeka as a home for student essays is a very easy way to shake up the traditional research essay. Students can write a fairly traditional essay in Microsoft Word or whatever they like, and then copy and paste the essay into an Omeka exhibit. It's a very easy process, but it can re-contextualize the essay from something that only the student and their professor and/or TF will ever read to something that exists out in the world with a wider potential audience. It also opens the door for the use of images or other multimedia additions to the essay, which can be a tremendous help in essays like this one on Japanese traditional pottery with deep Korean roots.
Another way to use Omeka in the classroom is to use its focus on collections of things as a platform to base other assignments off of. In this class, First Empires: Power and Propaganda in the Ancient World, the instructor curated this collection of objects for the students.
The students were given some basic information about the object, which they then had to use as the basis for their research into the object, and what meaning canbe derived from it.
These essays also live in the Omeka site, where they can incorporate other images and multimedia elements, and also include direct connections to those original objects.
A side benefit of using Omeka, from this same class, is that once students are already working in the system, they can make use of the additional functionality of its plugins, should they choose. This student used the Neatline mapping plugin to plot out differing interpretations of this eighth campaign of Sargon the second. What would be a somewhat ugly image in a traditional journal is here an interactive experience, where the audience can dig deep into the modern day terrain and see the relationship of the text describing the place to the varying interpretations of place.

Items

Items are the things that you will collect on your site. I'm being intentionally vague with my vocabulary, because you could just as easily have a collection of buildings as you could a collection of paintings or photographs. Anything that you might care to represent digitally can be accommodated, since all you need is a bit of metadata.

Dublin Core

Title Subject Description Creator Source
Publisher Date Contributor Rights Relation
Format Language Type Identifier Coverage
In order to have a setup that can accommodate any kind of "thing" while still having some degree of specificity and interoperability in the metadata used, Omeka uses a metadata standard called Dublin Core. You see here all of the metadata elements that are included in the standard. There are 15 of them, and none of them are required to create an item. Title and description are used frequently by Omeka, though, so it's a good idea to include them. Otherwise, you have a collection of 15 buckets that are general enough for you to put in whatever information you'd like. This generality makes it easier to use Omeka for any kind of material, because you aren't stuck with "author" or "artist", but instead the more general "creator", that can encompass both.
Files are also an important part of an Omeka item. Any file type can be added to an Omeka item, as long as the site's security settings have been set up to allow it. Only certain image, video and audio formats will be rendered on the page, all other formats will simply be downloadable.

Collections

Think boxes of items, described with the same Dublin Core metadata

Items can't go in multiple collections

Colllections are like boxes in an archive. They expect to have a logic to them, a reason to collect them in the same place. Because of this assumption, they have their own Dublin Core metadata that can be assigned. Also, Items can't be placed in multiple collections. They are like physical boxes, in that you can't put the same thing in two different boxes. For a less archival way of associating Items with each other, Omeka also offers a basic tagging system, with fewer descriptions and fewer restrictions.

Exhibits

Okay, let's move on to another core part of Omeka, the exhibit.
Exhibits are the narratives at the heart of an Omeka site. The items are fascinating on their own, but the exhibits pull together these materials into a narrative, and use them to substantiate that narrative.
  • Exhibit (http://base.url/exhibit-name)
    • Page (http://base.url/exhibit-name/page1)
      •   Block
      •   Block
      •   Block
    • Page (http://base.url/exhibit-name/page2)
      •   Block
Here's a bit about the structure of an Exhibit. At the top, you have the whole exhibit, which gets its own base url, which usually contains the exhibit's description. Beneath that, we have individual exhibit pages, which also get their own urls. These are the sections of an exhibit, which can be navigated through at the will of the reader. Finally, making up the pages, are the actual blocks of content. These are the bits of styled content, whether they're images, text, or other kinds of content blocks added by plugins. They don't get their own urls, because they're part of each page. You might have a page with some text, an image, and then a gallery of images, or you might have a page that's just a block of text; you can have as many or as few as you like.

Your turn (again)

The best way to cement that idea of how exhibits work is to try it out for yourself. Take your item and put it in an exhibit, with a bit of text about why you chose it to bring in to the site. Go nuts if you want to, but start there.

Extra features

There are some additional features of Omeka, added through plugins, which I'll go over now.

Neatline

Neatline is a plugin that lets you create interactive maps, annotated however you like to create a narrative.
Let's take a closer look at the map that that student created. We can zoom in and see the different paths, but on the right, we can also look through a translated narrative, annotated with links to the places described. You can also hover over each line, and see the name of the scholar who proposed that route for Sargon the second.
Here is another map, but you might notice that this one looks a little different. It's not a modern map, and it's not superimposed on the modern landscape, it's just an annotated image. You can do this with any image, making Neatline a viable annotation platform as well. It's worth mentioning that Neatline can do a lot, but it has a kind of clunky interface. It's still one of the best interfaces for creating quick and easy non-linear narrative maps, but that's because there's nothing particularly satisfying out there.

IIIF Toolkit

The IIIF Toolkit allows you to re-use images from other image hosting systems, like the library here or the Harvard Art Museums.
So IIIF stands for the International Image Interoperability Framework, and basically its a pretty technical standard for transmitting images. It makes it so that different repositories can speak the same, quite expressive language about their image content. In practical terms, this means that you don't have to re-host images, and you get some really great tiling that allows for zooming, panning, slicing and dicing of images. So not only is it easier to take advantage of the high quality images in archival repositories, but you also get some great features too.
